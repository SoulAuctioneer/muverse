The Big Question

Every quantum event splits reality into branches. But why do some branches matter more?

In the Many-Worlds Interpretation of quantum mechanics, every measurement causes the universe to split. Flip a coin and both "heads" and "tails" actually happen — in different branches of reality.

The Born rule tells us the probability of being in each branch: it's proportional to |ψ|², the square of the quantum wave function. But no one has explained why this rule works. It's just assumed.

The Key Idea

The multiverse has a finite energy budget

Think of it like an ecosystem. Life on Earth is governed by finite sunlight, finite water, finite nutrients. Organisms compete for resources, and the fittest survive.

Thermodynamic Darwinism says the quantum multiverse works the same way. Branches that "cost" too much energy get suppressed. The result? The Born rule falls out naturally.
